\subsection{Filters as Homomorphisms}

both constant maps from~$2^X$ to~$ğŸš$ satisfy the given definition of a homomorphism of meet-semilattice, but the homomorphism with constant value~$0$ corresponds to the empty collection of subsets of~$X$, which is not a filter.

The source of this problem is that the given definition of a meet-semilattice is not correct (for our purposes):
we also need the existence of a greatest element.

\begin{definition}
	A poset~$S$ is a \defemph{meet-semilattice} if
	\begin{enumerate*}
%
		\item
			every two elements of~$S$ admit a meet, and
%
		\item
			the poset~$S$ admits a greatest element.

	\end{enumerate*}
	The meet of two elements~$x$ and~$y$ of~$S$ is denoted by~$x âˆ§ y$.
	The greatest element of~$S$ is denoted by~$1$ (or by~$âŠ¤$), so that~$x âˆ§ 1 = x$ for every~$x âˆˆ S$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
	Let~$S$ and~$T$ be two meet-semilattices.
	A map~$f$ from~$S$ to~$T$ is a \defemph{homomorphism of meet-semilattices} if~$f (x âˆ§ y) = (f x) âˆ§ (f y)$ for all~$x, y âˆˆ X$ and also~$f 1_S = 1_T$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
	Let~$P$ and~$Q$ be two posets.
	A map~$f$ from~$P$ to~$Q$ is \defemph{isotone} if~$f x â‰¤ f y$ for all~$x, y âˆˆ P$ with~$x â‰¤ y$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}
	Every homomorphism of meet-semilattices is isotone.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
	Let~$S$ and~$T$ be two meet-semilattices and let~$f$ be a homomorphism of meet-semilattices from~$S$ to~$T$.
	For every two elements~$x$ and~$y$ of~$S$ we have the chain of equivalences and implications
	\[
		x â‰¤ y
		\iff
		x âˆ§ y = x
		\implies
		(f x) âˆ§ (f y) = f x
		\iff
		f x â‰¤ f y \,.
	\]
	This shows that~$f$ is isotone.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}
	Let~$S$ be a meet-semilattice.
	A subset of~$S$ in a \defemph{filter} if it is nonempty, downward directed and upward closed.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}
	Let~$S$ be a meet-semilattice.
	A subset~$F$ of~$S$ is a filter if and only if~$1 âˆˆ F$ and if we have for every two elements~$x$ and~$y$ of~$S$ the equivalence
	\[
		x âˆ§ y âˆˆ F \iff \text{$x âˆˆ F$ and~$y âˆˆ F$} \,.
	\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
	Suppose that~$F$ is a filter.
	This entails that~$F$ is upward closed and nonempty, whence~$1$ is contained in~$F$.
	If~$x âˆ§ y âˆˆ F$ then also~$x âˆˆ F$ and~$y âˆˆ F$ because~$F$ is upward closed.
	If on the other hand~$x âˆˆ F$ and~$y âˆˆ F$ then~$x âˆ§ y âˆˆ F$ because~$F$ is downward directed and upward closed.

	Suppose not that~$1 âˆˆ F$ and that for every two elements~$x$ and~$y$ of~$F$, we have~$x âˆ§ y âˆˆ F$ if and only if~$x, y âˆˆ F$.
	We know from the condition~$1 âˆˆ F$ that~$F$ is nonempty, and the implication~$x, y âˆˆ F \implies x âˆ§ y âˆˆ F$ tells us that~$F$ is downward directed.
	Given~$x âˆˆ F$ and~$y âˆˆ S$ with~$x â‰¤ y$, we have~$x âˆ§ y = x âˆˆ F$ and therefore also~$y âˆˆ F$.
\end{proof}

\[

We have for the second condition the chain of equivalences
\begin{align*}

	\text{$[x âˆ§ y âˆˆ F \iff x, y âˆˆ F]$ for all~$x, y âˆˆ S$} \\
	\iff{}&

We hence find that~$F$ is a filter in~$S$ if and only if the corresponding map~$f$ from~$S$ to~$ğŸš$ is a homomorphism of meet-semilattices.
